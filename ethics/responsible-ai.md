# Responsible AI for Agile Coaches

> Using AI ethically, transparently, and without eroding the trust of your team.

---

## Why this matters

As Scrum Masters and Agile Coaches, trust is our primary asset. The way we use AI — and whether we're transparent about it — directly affects the psychological safety and trust we've built with our teams.

This isn't about being anti-AI. It's about using it in a way that strengthens rather than undermines the human work we do.

---

## Core principles

### 1. Transparency
Be open with your team about when AI has helped produce an artefact. You don't need to caveat every email — but if a retrospective synthesis or sprint summary was AI-assisted, saying so is good practice and models healthy AI literacy for the team.

### 2. You own the output
AI is a tool. Your name is on the artefact. Review, edit, and personalise everything before sharing. Generic AI output that the team can recognise as AI-generated erodes credibility.

### 3. Protect privacy
Never paste personally identifiable information (names, HR data, performance details, personal disclosures from 1:1s) into a consumer AI tool. Use fictional names or role titles when building prompts with real-team context.

### 4. AI amplifies, not replaces
AI is excellent at first drafts, synthesis, and structured output. It cannot replace the coaching relationship, the facilitation instinct, or the empathy needed in a retrospective. Use AI for the admin; show up fully for the people work.

### 5. Verify before you trust
AI can confidently produce plausible-sounding content that is wrong. Always apply your own expertise to review outputs — especially anything involving agile frameworks, certifications, or methodology claims.

---

## Practical guidelines

| Situation | Guideline |
|---|---|
| Writing a stakeholder update with AI | Fine — edit and personalise before sending |
| Synthesising retro notes with AI | Fine — review themes with the team before actioning |
| Pasting a real team member's name | Don't — use their role title instead |
| Sharing AI output without editing | Avoid — it's obvious and damages credibility |
| Using AI to write coaching feedback | Use only as a starting point — the relationship is yours |
| AI-summarising a sensitive 1:1 | Never — this is a significant trust violation |
| Asking AI "what should I do with my team" | Fine — but apply your own judgement to the response |

---

## Questions to ask yourself

Before using AI output:
- Would I be comfortable if my team knew this was AI-assisted?
- Have I added my own expertise and context on top of this?
- Does this reflect how I actually speak and think?
- Is there any sensitive information in what I pasted?

---

## A note on coaching authenticity

There is a real risk that AI makes coaching feel efficient at the cost of feeling genuine. A coaching question that took you 10 seconds to generate with AI may be technically good — but the relationship that makes the question land takes years to build.

Use AI for the craft. Invest your time in the relationship.
